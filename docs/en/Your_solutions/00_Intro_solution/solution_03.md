To determine how the gravitational force changes, we can compare the initial force to the new force after the variables are modified.

### 1. The Original Formula

The Universal Law of Gravitation is given by:


$$F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$$

### 2. Identify the Changes

According to the problem, the variables change as follows:

* **Masses are halved:** $m_1' = \frac{1}{2}m_1$ and $m_2' = \frac{1}{2}m_2$
* **Distance is doubled:** $r' = 2r$

### 3. Substitute the New Values

Now, we plug these new values into the gravitational formula to find the new force, $F'$:


$$F' = G \frac{(\frac{1}{2}m_1) (\frac{1}{2}m_2)}{(2r)^2}$$

### 4. Simplify the Expression

First, simplify the numerator (the masses):


$$(\frac{1}{2}m_1) \times (\frac{1}{2}m_2) = \frac{1}{4} m_1 m_2$$

Next, simplify the denominator (the distance squared):


$$(2r)^2 = 4r^2$$

Now, put them back into the equation:


$$F' = G \frac{\frac{1}{4} m_1 m_2}{4r^2}$$

To simplify the fraction, divide the numerator's coefficient by the denominator's coefficient:


$$F' = \frac{1/4}{4} \times \left( G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} \right)$$

$$F' = \frac{1}{16} \times \left( G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} \right)$$

### 5. Compare the New Force to the Original

Since the expression in the parentheses is our original force ($F$), we can write:


$$F' = \frac{1}{16} F$$

### Final Result:

The gravitational force **decreases by a factor of 16** (or becomes $\frac{1}{16}$ of its original value).
