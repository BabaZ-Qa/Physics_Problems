To find the area under the curve of $f(x) = \sin(x)$ from $x=0$ to $x=\pi$, we use the definite integral.

### 1. Set Up the Integral

The area $A$ is defined by the integral of the function over the given interval:


$$A = \int_{0}^{\pi} \sin(x) \, dx$$

### 2. Find the Antiderivative

To solve the integral, we need the antiderivative of $\sin(x)$. Recall that the derivative of $\cos(x)$ is $-\sin(x)$, so the antiderivative of $\sin(x)$ is $-\cos(x)$:


$$\int \sin(x) \, dx = -\cos(x)$$

### 3. Apply the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

We evaluate the antiderivative at the upper limit ($\pi$) and subtract the value at the lower limit ($0$):


$$A = \left[ -\cos(x) \right]_{0}^{\pi}$$

$$A = (-\cos(\pi)) - (-\cos(0))$$

### 4. Evaluate the Trigonometric Values

Substitute the known values for $\cos(\pi)$ and $\cos(0)$:

* $\cos(\pi) = -1$
* $\cos(0) = 1$

Now, plug these into the equation:


$$A = (-(-1)) - (-(1))$$

$$A = 1 - (-1)$$

$$A = 1 + 1$$

### Final Result:

The area under the curve is:
**$A = 2$**

This means that one single "hump" of the sine curve (from $0$ to $\pi$) covers exactly 2 square units of area.
