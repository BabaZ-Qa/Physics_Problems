To calculate the centripetal acceleration of someone standing on the equator, we need to determine how fast they are moving due to the Earth's rotation. Even though we feel stationary, we are actually hurtling through space in a giant circle.

---

### 1. Identify the Given Values
* **Radius of the Earth ($R$):** $6378 \text{ km} = 6,378,000 \text{ m}$ (or $6.378 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$)
* **Period of Rotation ($T$):** One full rotation takes **24 hours**.



---

### 2. Convert the Period to Seconds
To use standard SI units (meters and seconds), we must convert the 24-hour day into seconds:
$$T = 24 \text{ hours} \times 60 \text{ minutes/hour} \times 60 \text{ seconds/minute}$$
$$T = 86,400 \text{ s}$$

---

### 3. Calculate Angular Velocity ($\omega$)
The angular velocity represents how many radians the Earth rotates per second. Since one full circle is $2\pi$ radians:
$$\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}$$
$$\omega = \frac{2 \times 3.14159}{86,400}$$
$$\omega \approx 7.272 \times 10^{-5} \text{ rad/s}$$

---

### 4. Calculate Centripetal Acceleration ($a_c$)
Now we use the formula for centripetal acceleration in terms of angular velocity and radius:
$$a_c = \omega^2 R$$

Plugging in our values:
$$a_c = (7.272 \times 10^{-5} \text{ rad/s})^2 \times (6,378,000 \text{ m})$$
$$a_c \approx (5.288 \times 10^{-9}) \times (6,378,000)$$
$$a_c \approx \mathbf{0.0337 \text{ m/s}^2}$$

---

### 5. Putting it into Perspective
To see how significant this is, let’s compare it to the acceleration due to gravity ($g \approx 9.81 \text{ m/s}^2$):
$$\frac{a_c}{g} = \frac{0.0337}{9.81} \approx 0.00343$$

**Conclusion:** The centripetal acceleration at the equator is approximately **$0.0337 \text{ m/s}^2$**. This is only about **$0.34\%$** of the force of gravity. This small outward "push" is why you would weigh slightly less at the equator than at the North Pole, but it's not nearly enough to overcome gravity and send you flying into the atmosphere!
