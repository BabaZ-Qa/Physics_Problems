To find the period of a pendulum in different environments, we rely on the relationship between length, gravity, and time.

The fundamental formula for the period ($T$) of a simple pendulum is:
$$T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$



---

### 1. Calculating Period on the Moon

**Step A: Establish the relationship between Earth and Moon**
We are given that $T_{Earth} = 4 \text{ s}$ and $g_{Moon} = \frac{1}{6}g_{Earth}$.
Since $T$ is inversely proportional to the square root of $g$ ($T \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{g}}$), we can set up a ratio:

$$\frac{T_{Moon}}{T_{Earth}} = \frac{2\pi \sqrt{L / g_{Moon}}}{2\pi \sqrt{L / g_{Earth}}}$$
$$\frac{T_{Moon}}{T_{Earth}} = \sqrt{\frac{g_{Earth}}{g_{Moon}}}$$

**Step B: Substitute the values**
$$\frac{T_{Moon}}{4} = \sqrt{\frac{g_{Earth}}{g_{Earth} / 6}}$$
$$\frac{T_{Moon}}{4} = \sqrt{6}$$

**Step C: Solve for $T_{Moon}$**
$$T_{Moon} = 4 \times \sqrt{6}$$
Using $\sqrt{6} \approx 2.45$:
$$T_{Moon} \approx 4 \times 2.45 = \mathbf{9.80 \text{ s}}$$

**Interpretation:** Because the Moon's gravity is much weaker, the "restoring force" pulling the pendulum back is smaller, making the swing significantly slower.

---

### 2. Required Length for a 1-Second Period on Earth

Now we need to find the specific length ($L$) required to achieve a period of exactly $1 \text{ second}$ on Earth (using $g \approx 9.81 \text{ m/s}^2$).

**Step A: Rearrange the formula for $L$**
$$T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$
$$\frac{T}{2\pi} = \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$
$$\frac{T^2}{4\pi^2} = \frac{L}{g}$$
$$L = \frac{g T^2}{4\pi^2}$$

**Step B: Substitute the values ($T=1$, $g=9.81$)**
$$L = \frac{9.81 \times (1)^2}{4\pi^2}$$
$$L = \frac{9.81}{4 \times 9.8696}$$
$$L \approx \frac{9.81}{39.478}$$
$$L \approx \mathbf{0.2485 \text{ m}} \text{ (or } \mathbf{24.85 \text{ cm}})$$

---

### Summary
* **Period on the Moon:** $\approx 9.80 \text{ s}$
* **Required Length on Earth:** $\approx 24.85 \text{ cm}$

Interestingly, a pendulum with a $2 \text{-second}$ period (known as a "seconds pendulum" because each "tick" or half-swing is exactly one second) requires a length of about $1 \text{ meter}$.
