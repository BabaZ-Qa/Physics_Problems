This problem involves two distinct phases: first, the **Conservation of Energy** as the block slides down the track, and second, the **Conservation of Momentum** during the collision.

### Step 1: Energy Conversion (The Slide)
As the $0.5 \text{ kg}$ block slides down the frictionless track, its gravitational potential energy ($PE$) is converted entirely into kinetic energy ($KE$) at the bottom.



* **Mass of block 1 ($m_1$):** $0.5 \text{ kg}$
* **Height ($h$):** $3.0 \text{ m}$
* **Gravity ($g$):** $9.81 \text{ m/s}^2$

Using the conservation of energy equation:
$$m_1gh = \frac{1}{2}m_1v_1^2$$

The mass $m_1$ cancels out, and we solve for the velocity $v_1$ at the bottom:
$$v_1 = \sqrt{2gh}$$
$$v_1 = \sqrt{2 \cdot 9.81 \cdot 3.0}$$
$$v_1 = \sqrt{58.86}$$
**$v_1 \approx 7.67 \text{ m/s}$**

---

### Step 2: Momentum Conservation (The Collision)
At the bottom, the first block ($m_1$) hits the second block ($m_2 = 1.5 \text{ kg}$) which is at rest ($v_2 = 0$). Since they stick together, this is a **perfectly inelastic collision**.



According to the Law of Conservation of Momentum:
$$P_{before} = P_{after}$$
$$m_1v_1 + m_2v_2 = (m_1 + m_2)v_f$$

Substitute the known values:
$$(0.5 \cdot 7.67) + (1.5 \cdot 0) = (0.5 + 1.5)v_f$$
$$3.835 = 2.0 \cdot v_f$$

Now, solve for the final velocity ($v_f$):
$$v_f = \frac{3.835}{2.0}$$
**$v_f \approx 1.92 \text{ m/s}$**
