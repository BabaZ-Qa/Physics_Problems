To solve for the height after the second bounce, we need to track the mechanical energy of the tennis ball as it decreases by 30% after each impact.

### 1. Identify Initial Energy
When the ball is at its initial height ($h_0 = 2.0 \text{ m}$), all of its mechanical energy is potential energy ($PE$):
$$E_0 = mgh_0$$

### 2. Energy After the First Bounce
The problem states the ball loses 30% of its energy. This means it **retains 70%** (or $0.70$) of its energy after each bounce.



Let $E_1$ be the energy after the first bounce:
$$E_1 = 0.70 \cdot E_0$$
$$E_1 = 0.70 \cdot (mgh_0)$$

Since the energy at the peak of the first bounce is also potential energy ($mgh_1$), we can write:
$$mgh_1 = 0.70 \cdot mgh_0$$

The mass ($m$) and gravity ($g$) cancel out, leaving:
$$h_1 = 0.70 \cdot h_0$$
$$h_1 = 0.70 \cdot 2.0 \text{ m} = 1.4 \text{ m}$$

---

### 3. Energy After the Second Bounce
After the second impact, the ball again retains only 70% of the energy it had just before that impact ($E_1$):
$$E_2 = 0.70 \cdot E_1$$

Using the same height relationship as before:
$$h_2 = 0.70 \cdot h_1$$
$$h_2 = 0.70 \cdot 1.4 \text{ m}$$
**$h_2 = 0.98 \text{ m}$**

---

### Summary of the Bouncing Process

| Bounce Number | Energy Retained | Calculation | Peak Height |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **0 (Drop)** | $100\%$ | $2.0 \text{ m}$ | $2.0 \text{ m}$ |
| **1st Bounce** | $70\%$ | $0.70 \cdot 2.0$ | $1.4 \text{ m}$ |
| **2nd Bounce** | $49\%$ ($0.7^2$) | $0.70 \cdot 1.4$ | **$0.98 \text{ m}$** |



**Result:** After the second bounce, the tennis ball rises to a height of **$0.98 \text{ meters}$**.
