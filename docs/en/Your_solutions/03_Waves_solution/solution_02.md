To find the speed of the wave on a string, we look at the relationship between the length of the string and the wavelength of its fundamental frequency.

### 1. Identify the Wavelength ($\lambda$)
For a string fixed at both ends, the fundamental frequency (the first harmonic) forms a standing wave with **one antinode** in the center and two nodes at the ends. This means the length of the string ($L$) is equal to exactly half of a wavelength:

$$L = \frac{\lambda}{2} \quad \text{or} \quad \lambda = 2L$$

**Given:**
*   Length ($L$) = 64 cm = **0.64 m** (converted to meters for standard units)

**Calculation:**
$$\lambda = 2 \times 0.64\text{ m}$$
$$\lambda = 1.28\text{ m}$$

---

### 2. Calculate the Wave Speed ($v$)
Now we use the wave equation to find the velocity:

$$v = f \cdot \lambda$$

**Given:**
*   Fundamental frequency ($f$) = 330 Hz
*   Wavelength ($\lambda$) = 1.28 m

**Calculation:**
$$v = 330\text{ Hz} \times 1.28\text{ m}$$
$$v = 422.4\text{ m/s}$$

---

### Summary
*   **Wavelength:** 1.28 m
*   **Wave Speed:** 422.4 m/s

On a standard guitar, this speed is influenced by the tension of the string and its linear mass density. If you were to tighten the tuning peg, the speed (and thus the frequency) would increase!
