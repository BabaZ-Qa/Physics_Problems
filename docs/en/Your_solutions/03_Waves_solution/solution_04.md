To find the phase difference, we look at the relationship between the spatial distance (path difference) and the angular displacement of the wave.

### 1. Understand the Relationship
A full wavelength ($\lambda$) represents one complete cycle of a wave. In terms of phase, one complete cycle is equal to $2\pi$ radians (or $360^\circ$).

Therefore, the phase difference ($\Delta\phi$) is proportional to the ratio of the separation distance ($\Delta x$) to the total wavelength ($\lambda$). The formula is:

$$\Delta\phi = 2\pi \left( \frac{\Delta x}{\lambda} \right)$$

---

### 2. Substitute the Given Values
**Given:**
*   Separation distance ($\Delta x$) = $\frac{\lambda}{3}$

**Calculation:**
$$\Delta\phi = 2\pi \left( \frac{\lambda / 3}{\lambda} \right)$$

The $\lambda$ terms cancel out:
$$\Delta\phi = 2\pi \left( \frac{1}{3} \right)$$
$$\Delta\phi = \frac{2\pi}{3} \text{ radians}$$

---

### 3. Final Result
The phase difference is **$\frac{2\pi}{3}$ radians**.

> **Pro-tip:** If you need to visualize this in degrees, you can convert it by substituting $\pi = 180^\circ$:
> $$\Delta\phi = \frac{2(180^\circ)}{3} = 120^\circ$$
> This means the second point is exactly one-third of a cycle behind (or ahead of) the first point.
