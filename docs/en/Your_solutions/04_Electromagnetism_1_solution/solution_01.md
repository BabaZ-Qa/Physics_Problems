To calculate the total force on the center charge, we use **Coulomb's Law** and the principle of superposition.

### 1. Geometry of the Square
First, we determine the distance ($r$) from each corner to the center of the square.
*   The diagonal of a square with side $s = 1.0\text{ m}$ is $d = s\sqrt{2} = \sqrt{2}\text{ m}$.
*   The distance from a corner to the center is half the diagonal:
$$r = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\text{ m} \approx 0.707\text{ m}$$

### 2. Force from a Single Corner Charge
All four corner charges ($q_1$) are identical ($+1.0\text{ C}$), and the center charge ($q_2$) is $-2.0\text{ C}$. The force between one corner and the center is attractive because the charges have opposite signs.

Using Coulomb's Law ($k \approx 8.99 \times 10^9 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{C}^2$):
$$F = k \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$$
$$F = (8.99 \times 10^9) \frac{|(1.0)( -2.0)|}{(\sqrt{2}/2)^2}$$
$$F = (8.99 \times 10^9) \frac{2}{0.5}$$
$$F = 3.596 \times 10^{10} \text{ N}$$

### 3. Vector Superposition
Now we consider the direction of the four force vectors acting on the center charge:
*   **Top-Left Charge:** Pulls the center charge toward the top-left corner.
*   **Bottom-Right Charge:** Pulls the center charge toward the bottom-right corner.
*   **Top-Right Charge:** Pulls the center charge toward the top-right corner.
*   **Bottom-Left Charge:** Pulls the center charge toward the bottom-left corner.

Since the center charge is exactly in the middle and the four corner charges are equal in magnitude:
1.  The force from the **top-left** charge is equal and opposite to the force from the **bottom-right** charge. These two cancel each other out.
2.  The force from the **top-right** charge is equal and opposite to the force from the **bottom-left** charge. These two also cancel each other out.

### 4. Final Result
Because the system is perfectly symmetrical, the vector sum of the forces is:
$$\vec{F}_{net} = \vec{F}_1 + \vec{F}_2 + \vec{F}_3 + \vec{F}_4 = 0$$

*   **Magnitude:** $0\text{ N}$
*   **Direction:** None (The charge is in equilibrium)

> **Insight:** This is a classic example of electrostatic symmetry. No matter how large the charges are, if they are placed symmetrically around a central point, the net electric field (and thus the force) at that center point will be zero.
