To find the equilibrium position, we need to locate the point where the net electric force on $q_3$ is zero. This occurs when the repulsive force from $q_1$ is exactly equal in magnitude and opposite in direction to the repulsive force from $q_2$.

### 1. Set Up the Coordinates
Let’s place the charges on an x-axis:
*   Charge $q_1 = +4\text{ C}$ is at $x = 0$.
*   Charge $q_2 = +9\text{ C}$ is at $x = 2\text{ m}$.
*   Charge $q_3 = +1\text{ C}$ is at some unknown position $x$ between them.

The distance from $q_1$ to $q_3$ is $x$.
The distance from $q_2$ to $q_3$ is $(2 - x)$.



---

### 2. Equate the Forces
According to Coulomb's Law, the force $F$ is $k \frac{q_a q_b}{r^2}$. For $q_3$ to be in equilibrium:
$$F_{13} = F_{23}$$

Substitute the values:
$$k \frac{q_1 q_3}{x^2} = k \frac{q_2 q_3}{(2 - x)^2}$$

We can cancel $k$ and $q_3$ from both sides, as they are non-zero:
$$\frac{q_1}{x^2} = \frac{q_2}{(2 - x)^2}$$

---

### 3. Solve for $x$
Plug in the values for $q_1$ and $q_2$:
$$\frac{4}{x^2} = \frac{9}{(2 - x)^2}$$

To make this easier to solve, take the **square root** of both sides:
$$\sqrt{\frac{4}{x^2}} = \sqrt{\frac{9}{(2 - x)^2}}$$
$$\frac{2}{x} = \frac{3}{2 - x}$$

Now, cross-multiply:
$$2(2 - x) = 3x$$
$$4 - 2x = 3x$$
$$4 = 5x$$
$$x = \frac{4}{5} = 0.8\text{ m}$$

---

### Final Result
The equilibrium position for $q_3$ is **$0.8\text{ m}$ away from the $+4\text{ C}$ charge** (and $1.2\text{ m}$ away from the $+9\text{ C}$ charge).

> **Logic Check:** It makes sense that the equilibrium point is closer to the smaller charge ($q_1$) because $q_1$ has a weaker "push." The third charge must be closer to it to compensate for its lower magnitude compared to $q_2$.
