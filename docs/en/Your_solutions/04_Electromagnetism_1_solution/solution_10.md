To calculate the magnetic force on a current-carrying wire, we use the formula for the **Lorentz force on a conductor**:

$$F = I \cdot L \cdot B \cdot \sin(\theta)$$

**Where:**
*   $I$ is the current (**10 A**)
*   $L$ is the length of the wire (**2.0 m**)
*   $B$ is the magnetic field strength (**0.5 T**)
*   $\theta$ is the angle between the wire and the field lines

---

### 1. Identify the Constant Factor ($I \cdot L \cdot B$)
Before solving for the specific angles, we can calculate the base value of the force:
$$F_{base} = (10\text{ A}) \cdot (2.0\text{ m}) \cdot (0.5\text{ T})$$
$$F_{base} = 10\text{ N}$$

Now, we simply multiply this base value by the sine of each given angle.

---

### 2. Solve for Specific Angles

#### **a) $\theta = 90^\circ$ (Perpendicular)**
When the wire is perpendicular to the field, the force is at its maximum because $\sin(90^\circ) = 1$.
$$F = 10\text{ N} \cdot \sin(90^\circ)$$
$$F = 10 \cdot 1 = \mathbf{10\text{ N}}$$

#### **b) $\theta = 45^\circ$**
At a $45^\circ$ angle, we use $\sin(45^\circ) = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \approx 0.707$.
$$F = 10\text{ N} \cdot \sin(45^\circ)$$
$$F = 10 \cdot 0.707 = \mathbf{7.07\text{ N}}$$

#### **c) $\theta = 0^\circ$ (Parallel)**
When the wire is parallel to the magnetic field lines, $\sin(0^\circ) = 0$.
$$F = 10\text{ N} \cdot \sin(0^\circ)$$
$$F = 10 \cdot 0 = \mathbf{0\text{ N}}$$

---

### Summary Table

| Angle ($\theta$) | Calculation | Force ($F$) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **$90^\circ$** | $10 \cdot 1.0$ | **10.0 N** |
| **$45^\circ$** | $10 \cdot 0.707$ | **7.07 N** |
| **$0^\circ$** | $10 \cdot 0$ | **0.0 N** |

> **Key takeaway:** A magnetic field exerts no force on a wire if the current is flowing parallel to the field lines. The "push" only happens when there is a perpendicular component to the motion of the charges.
